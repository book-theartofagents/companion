Challenge 14: Write the framework obituary
===========================================
Paired with Chapter 14 (Interlude: Terrain Shifts) and chapters/14-interlude-terrain-shifts/

Context
-------
Chapter 14 is the interlude that looks at framework ecology. LangChain,
LangGraph, AWS Strands, Dify, LlamaIndex, Vanna, Temporal, Instructor,
Outlines, BAML, MCP. Each is useful; each will be replaced. The
example in chapters/14-interlude-terrain-shifts/ runs a small survey
of the April 2026 landscape and shows what to keep when the names
change.

Challenge
---------
1. Run `python chapters/14-interlude-terrain-shifts/run-eval.py`.
   Read the survey output.
2. Pick one framework you used two years ago that is now dormant or
   replaced. Write its obituary in three sentences: what problem it
   solved, why it lost, what the replacement kept.
3. Pick one framework from this chapter you believe will be
   replaced within 18 months. Justify in one sentence.
4. Now the harder step: identify the invariant underneath both the
   old and the new framework. That invariant is the thing to keep
   in your head; everything else is vocabulary.
5. Write the invariant as a one-line sentence that would still be
   true in 2030.

Constraints
-----------
- Your invariant must be a claim about systems, not about vendors.
  "You need logs" is an invariant; "you need Datadog" is not.
- No obituary longer than three sentences.

Success criterion
-----------------
One obituary, one prediction, one invariant. Tape the invariant
above your monitor. If you find yourself rewriting it in a year,
you had it wrong.

Where to compare
----------------
If your invariant reads like a product description, it is too
specific. If it reads like "software should be good", it is too
abstract. Aim for mid-altitude: specific enough to act on, general
enough to survive a rewrite.
